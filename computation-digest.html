Intel reportedly says it boosted yields by selling what would normally be 'scrap' or 'low-expectation' CPUs

Tom's Hardware
  • Intel confirmed that AI-driven CPU demand has been so strong that customers are willingly accepting chips that would previously have been binned or discarded — a notable shift in yield economics.
  • This effectively raises Intel's 'acceptable yield' floor, converting what was previously lost revenue into shipped product, which materially improves fab economics without any process improvements.
  • The practice raises questions about binning transparency: end users may not know they're receiving lower-binned parts when demand outstrips supply of premium silicon.
  • It's a candid admission of supply chain stress — the kind of disclosure that typically only surfaces at investor relations events under pressure.

CEO Interview with Xianxin Guo of Lumai — optical computing for AI acceleration

SemiWiki
  • Lumai is an Oxford spin-out pursuing photonic computing for AI inference and data center acceleration — targeting the fundamental power and bandwidth wall that electronic ASICs face at scale.
  • Optical computing can perform matrix multiplications at the speed of light with near-zero energy cost for the compute itself; the engineering challenge is the analog-to-digital conversion at the periphery.

Hot take: AI's not going to kill open source code security

The Register
  • Cal.com's switch from AGPL-3.0 to a closed commercial license re-ignites the debate about whether AI training on open source code creates a sustainable exploitation loop that incentivizes relicensing.
  • The argument that AI will 'kill' open source security is countered by the reality that OSS security benefits from more eyeballs — AI doesn't remove contributors, it may add automated ones.
  • The real threat is economic: if AI enables companies to extract value from OSS without contributing back, the funding and motivation for maintainers erodes over time, which is a sustainability problem more than a security one.
