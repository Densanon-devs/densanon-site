Intel's upcoming 42-core Nova Lake SKU allegedly upgraded to 44 cores

Nvidia Pascal GPUs debuted 10 years ago today

Tom's Hardware
  • Pascal launched on April 5, 2016 with the Tesla P100, introducing TSMC 16nm FinFET to Nvidia's datacenter and consumer GPU lines — the first major node shrink since Maxwell on 28nm.
  • The GTX 1080 Ti became one of the most long-lived enthusiast GPUs in history, remaining competitive well into the Turing generation and serving as the benchmark baseline for years.
  • Pascal's HBM2 debut on the P100 and NVLink interconnect were the architectural seeds of the datacenter GPU strategy that Nvidia now dominates with Hopper and Blackwell.

PlayStation 3 emulator makes Cell CPU 'breakthrough'

Tom's Hardware
  • RPCS3 developers have achieved a significant Cell Broadband Engine emulation breakthrough that improves performance across all PS3 titles, not just specific games.
  • The Cell BE's heterogeneous SPU architecture — eight independent Synergistic Processing Units managed by a Power PC core — has made it one of the most technically challenging CPUs ever emulated.
  • The improvement has broad implications for PS3 preservation: previously unplayable or severely degraded titles move closer to playable, and it demonstrates how much untapped headroom remained in the emulator's SPU dispatch path.

Hobbyist builds a homebrew Intel 8086 ISA accelerator card

Tom's Hardware
  • A maker engineered a custom ISA accelerator card that slots into 8086-class systems and offloads integer multiplication to dedicated hardware, delivering a 250% throughput improvement on an operation that was notoriously slow on the original 8086.
  • The 8086's multiply instruction took 70–160 clock cycles depending on operand size; dedicated co-processor logic on modern FPGAs or ASICs can collapse this to a handful of cycles, and this project bridges that gap for real vintage hardware.
  • The project is fully documented and represents a growing trend of FPGA-backed ISA expansion cards that extend classic PC hardware beyond its original design constraints.

Linux 7.1 To Expose AMD Zen 6's AVX-512 BMM For Guest VMs

Phoronix
  • Linux 7.1 will expose AMD Zen 6's AVX-512 BMM (Bit Manipulation Matrix) extension to KVM guests, making a new class of bit-manipulation acceleration available to virtualized workloads.
  • AVX-512 BMM accelerates sparse matrix and bitfield operations that appear in compression, cryptography, and certain ML inference pipelines — guest VMs running these workloads on Zen 6 hosts will benefit without requiring passthrough.
  • The patch continues Linux's practice of rapidly surfacing new x86 ISA extensions to hypervisor guests, keeping virtualized environments close to bare-metal capability parity on new silicon.

Mesa 26.1 Makes It Easier To 'Fake' A GPU Reset Using LLVMpipe

Phoronix
  • Mesa 26.1 adds a mechanism to simulate GPU reset events using LLVMpipe, allowing compositor and display server developers to test GPU recovery paths without triggering real hardware resets.
  • GPU reset handling is notoriously difficult to test because real resets are rare, hardware-dependent, and destructive — a software-controllable fake reset lowers the barrier to robust recovery code.
  • The addition benefits Wayland compositor developers in particular, where GPU reset handling has been an underspecified and undertested code path across multiple compositors.

hid-omg-detect: Linux Driver In Development To Detect Malicious HID Devices

Phoronix
  • A new Linux kernel driver, hid-omg-detect, is being developed to identify O.MG Cable-style malicious HID devices that masquerade as keyboards or mice to inject arbitrary input.
  • HID-based attack vectors are particularly dangerous because USB HID is inherently trusted by the OS — this driver adds a detection layer that can flag anomalous device behavior indicative of an implant.
  • Targeting O.MG and similar red-team hardware at the kernel level is a meaningful shift toward supply chain and physical access security being handled in-kernel rather than relying solely on endpoint agents.

How Nvidia learned to embrace the light in its quest for scale

The Register
  • Nvidia is investing heavily in photonic interconnects to solve the bandwidth and power bottlenecks that copper NVLink hits at rack scale — optical links enable scale-up topologies across 1000+ GPU clusters without the signal integrity limits of copper.
  • The shift from copper to silicon photonics for GPU-to-GPU communication represents a fundamental change in how AI supercomputer racks are architected, with Nvidia positioning its internal photonics efforts as a competitive moat.
  • Optical scale-up also reduces cooling complexity at the interconnect layer, a non-trivial benefit as power density in AI training clusters continues to push past 100kW per rack.
